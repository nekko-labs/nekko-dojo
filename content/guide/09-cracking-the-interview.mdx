You know the algorithms and the architecture. Now you practice **performing** under interview conditions, because interviewing is its own skill — separate from coding.

## Study: Cracking the Coding Interview

The classic resource. **Start with the YouTube material first** — it's faster and gives you the shape of things:

- **Cracking the Coding Interview — Gayle Laakmann McDowell (2012)** — the foundational talk from the book's author.
- **Cracking the Facebook Coding Interview — The Approach** — how to think through problems methodically.
- **How to: Work at Google — Example Coding/Engineering Interview** — watch a real mock interview play out.

Book alternative: **Cracking the Coding Interview, 6th Edition** (189 programming questions and solutions).

## Start mock interviewing

Reading is not practicing. Run mock interviews — and you can **practice these loops with me** in the community.

### The whiteboard (coding) process

- **Know your language and its syntax** cold — you don't want to fumble basics while thinking about the problem.
- **Ask questions** before you start coding. Clarify the inputs, outputs, and constraints.
- **Write readable code.** Interviewers are evaluating how you'd write code on their team, not just whether it runs.
- **Give your brute-force option first**, then improve it.
- **Be prepared for follow-ups:** scaling, Big-O analysis, and extra constraints (offline mode, two simultaneous users, etc.).

### The architecture whiteboard

- Draw a **black-box diagram** — boxes and arrows for the major components.
- Be ready for **database design** — sketch a **data model diagram** showing your entities and how their tables relate.
- Walk through it out loud, scaling from few users to many (see the [System Architecture](/guide/system-architecture) chapter).

### Behavioral questions

These matter as much as the technical loops. Prepare real stories for:

- What's your biggest failure? Your biggest accomplishment?
- Why should we hire you? How can you help our company?
- What are your goals?
- "Tell me about a time…" — a conflict with another person, a mistake you made, a time you felt confident in your work.

Have concrete examples ready; don't improvise these in the room.

## Practice architecture whiteboarding — for real

Start in a web editor, then move to a physical whiteboard (because that's where the real interview happens):

- **[Excalidraw](https://excalidraw.com/)**
- **[diagrams.net](https://www.diagrams.net/)**
- **[Miro](https://miro.com/)**

Then get up and **draw on an actual physical whiteboard.** Verbally talk through the problem, your questions, and your reasoning as you draw — exactly as you will on the day.

## Do this now

Watch the *How to: Work at Google* mock interview, then do your own: pick a LeetCode problem, stand at a whiteboard (or paper), and solve it **out loud** — clarify, brute-force, improve, analyze Big-O. Record yourself or do it with someone. When you're ready, book a mock loop with me in [the Discord](https://discord.gg/nekkolabs).
