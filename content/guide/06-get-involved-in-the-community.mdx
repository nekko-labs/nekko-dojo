This is the chapter that changes outcomes. Almost everyone who lands a job made this jump: from solo projects to contributing on a **real, production application** alongside other engineers.

## Why this is the unlock

Solo projects teach you to write code. But a job is mostly *not* writing code from scratch — it's working on a team, in a large codebase you didn't write, to someone else's standards. Open source gives you the real thing before anyone pays you:

- Reading and understanding code other people wrote
- Code review — giving it and receiving it
- Collaborating with Git: branches, pull requests, merge conflicts
- Shipping to real users who notice when it breaks

"Has contributed to a real team project" is one of the strongest signals you can send a hiring manager.

## It's daunting to start — so here's where we help

Finding a project and getting involved is intimidating. You don't have to figure it out alone. Here are a few projects to check out and get involved in:

- **Nekko Dojo** — yes, *this* project. It's open source, and you can help improve the very guide and site you're reading. A friendly first place to make a contribution.
- **Nekko Notes** — an AI-first, local-first notetaking app built by Nekko Labs. Real TypeScript, real product, real team.
- **[Find a Doc Japan](https://findadoc.jp)** — an open-source, NPO-run project in Japan (TypeScript/Vue/GraphQL) that helps people find healthcare in their language. It's deliberately welcoming to learners and has helped 225+ engineers grow by contributing — several landed jobs through it.

See the full curated list on the [Community & Projects](/community) page, and come to [the Discord](https://discord.gg/nekkolabs) to get paired with an issue that fits you.

## How to make your first contribution

1. **Lurk first.** Read the README and contributing guide. Look at a few recent pull requests to see how the team works.
2. **Find a small issue.** Look for a `good first issue` label. Fixing a typo or a small bug is a perfectly good — and welcome — start.
3. **Ask before you build.** Comment on the issue, say you'd like to take it, ask any questions. Communicating well *is* the skill being evaluated.
4. **Open a small pull request.** Small and finished beats large and perfect. Expect review feedback — that feedback *is* the learning.
5. **Iterate gracefully.** Respond to review, make the changes, say thank you. This is exactly what the job is, every day.

## Do this now

Pick one project above, find one `good first issue` (or ask in [the Discord](https://discord.gg/nekkolabs) for a pointer), and comment that you'd like to work on it. That single action puts you ahead of most people learning to code. Once it's merged, you have something almost no other junior applicant does: proof you can work on a real team.
